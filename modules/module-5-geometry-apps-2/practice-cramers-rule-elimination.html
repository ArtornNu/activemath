Elimination by Combining Lines

You already know how to solve two equations by graphing their lines and reading off where they cross. This time, try making a third line out of the first two — and see what happens when you make it perfectly flat.

No vocabulary up front. You'll get two sliders that blend the two equations together into a new line. Just try to make that new line as flat as you can, and see what you can read off when you do.

Reflection

What did you notice?

Look back at what you just did across Steps 2–4.

In your own words: when the gray line went perfectly vertical, what happened to the y in its equation? When it went perfectly flat, what happened to x?

You did this by sliding two numbers, k1 and k2. Is that different from what you'd do solving the same system with pencil and paper — multiplying each equation by something, then adding them together? How is it the same move?

Try writing your own answer first — then check it against this.

When the line stood perfectly vertical, the y-terms from the two equations had cancelled each other out — the combined equation had no y left, so only x remained to read off. Making it perfectly flat did the mirror-image thing to x instead: the x-terms cancelled, leaving only y.

It's the same move as pencil-and-paper elimination, not a different one. Sliding k1 and k2 is choosing the two multipliers you'd otherwise pick by hand before adding the two equations together — on paper you search for multipliers that cancel a variable; here you searched for the same thing, just guided by watching the line's angle instead of computing it directly.

For facilitatorsLet the room articulate "the y-terms cancelled" or similar before Slide 6 supplies the word "elimination." If a participant already sees the pencil-and-paper connection, that's the whole point of this activity landing early. The sample answer is intentionally collapsed by default — encourage participants to write or say their own version before revealing it.
Wrap-up

The general rule

What you just did by hand already has a name — elimination of variables.

Multiplying each equation by a number and adding them is exactly what k1 and k2 were doing — k1·(a1x+b1y) + k2·(a2x+b2y) = k1·c1 + k2·c2.

Making the line vertical means choosing k1, k2 so the y-terms cancel (k1·b1 + k2·b2 = 0) — exactly the "multiply then add to cancel a variable" method, just made visible as a line standing up straight instead of lying flat.

The k1, k2 values that do this are themselves ratios of the same kind of determinant used in the area version of this activity (if you've done it) — two completely different-looking routes to the same numbers.

For facilitatorsThis slide is deliberately the only place "elimination" is named plainly. If your group also did the area-ratio activity, this is the moment to ask which route felt more natural, and why both landed on the same answer.
