One Equation, Then a System

No word problem yet — just the bare numbers. Five short steps, each one adding a little more generality than the last.

You'll build up from the simplest possible equation to a full system of two equations — and see the same answer show up in two completely different-looking pictures.

Step 1 of 4

One equation, one unknown

2 × x = 8. Drag the green point until the blue point lands on the orange target.

🎯 Try dragging the green point
This is the simplest version of everything in this whole activity: an unknown scale, applied to something fixed, aiming at a target.
For facilitatorsEvery later idea in this module — basis vectors, matrix multiplication, systems of equations — is a generalization of exactly this one question. Worth saying out loud before moving on.
Step 2 of 4

One equation, TWO unknowns

2x + y = 8. Drag the point until 2x + y really does equal 8 — then keep going and find another spot that also works.

🎯 Try dragging the point onto the line
You already solved 2×x=8 — one unknown, one answer. Add a second unknown to the same kind of equation, and the answer set stretches from a single point into a whole line. Same idea, one more dimension.
For facilitators(4, 0) from Step 1 still works here too — it's just one point on this line now, no longer the only one. Worth letting participants notice that themselves before you say it.
Step 3 of 4

A SYSTEM of two equations

Now also require x + y = 5. Drag the point until BOTH lines pass through it at once.

🎯 Find where both lines cross
Nothing new here mathematically — graph both lines, read off where they cross. The second equation is what shrank "a whole line of answers" down to "exactly one answer."
For facilitatorsThis is the method already in every textbook. The payoff comes next: the exact same crossing point reappears from a totally different picture.
Step 4 of 4

The same system, read as vectors

Same system as Step 3 — but now drag on the left, and watch both pictures move from the same (x, y).

Lines crossing — drag to find (x, y)
Vectors combining (same x, y)
🎯 Try dragging the left-hand point
You're solving the exact same system two completely different-looking ways — intersecting lines, and combining vectors — and landing on the identical answer both times. That's one structure, seen from two angles.
For facilitatorsNoticing "same answer, two pictures" is what doing mathematics actually feels like — this is the moment to pause and let the room sit with it before moving on.
Wrap-up

A shortcut for what you just did

Every step above already IS the general rule — just written as a picture instead of a formula.

What you found by dragging is exactly: M · (x, y) = target, where M's rows are the two equations' coefficients and the target is their two totals.

det(M) — one number that says "exactly one solution?" with no drawing at all, and works the same whether there are 2 unknowns or 200.

M⁻¹ — solved once, it re-solves the very same system instantly for any new target, no re-graphing needed.

For facilitatorsThis slide is deliberately the only place the formula is stated plainly — every prior slide let participants find the pattern first. If a participant asks for the formula earlier, it's fine to point here rather than derail the step they're on.
