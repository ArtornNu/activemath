Wormhole Transverse · a companion game for Module 5

Enter the Construct

Picture a hidden treasure. You and a friend each have a map of the exact same yard — except your "steps" don't match at all. A Matrix is simply the recipe for turning your friend's steps into yours, and back again. Four ideas make up the whole game:

The Prime Construct — your normal map. Walk two straight steps — one this way, one that way — like an ordinary ruler, and every spot has an honest, easy address.
The Warp — your friend's map of that same yard. Their two steps are stretched and tilted, not straight at all — but they still work, as long as you know exactly how they're bent.
The Multiverse — every different stretch and tilt makes another "friend's map." They're not separate yards — just other ways of measuring the very same one.
The Bridge — turning an address on one map into an address on the other. That's the real puzzle you'll be solving, every single round.

Welcome, Operator. Across four Trials, you'll find one exact spot two different ways, then flip the problem around, and finally measure just how stretched your friend's map really is. No formulas are handed to you up front — you'll meet each one by doing the thing it describes, first.

For facilitatorsThis is a standalone companion for Module 5's matrix activities — best played after Activities 2, 3, 5 and 6, since each Trial quietly reuses one of those ideas (matching a shape, change of basis, area/determinant, inverse) without naming it up front. Works equally well as a warm-up recap or a closing consolidation game.

Debrief

What you just did, named

Trial I was matrix–vector multiplication: x = a·m + b·n, y = c·m + d·n.

Trial II was the inverse matrix M⁻¹ — undoing that same multiplication.

Trial III was the determinant, ad − bc — the factor by which the Warp stretches or shrinks area, and the exact number that decides whether Trial II even has an answer.

For facilitatorsConsider replaying Trial III as a group and deliberately hunting for a Glitch (det ≈ 0) and a Mirror Anomaly (det < 0) together — both are easy to trigger and make excellent whole-room discussion prompts about what a "structure" can and can't do.
