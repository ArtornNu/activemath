Reflection

What did you notice?

Look back at your table from Step 3.

Every single row, the ratio of areas matched the (x, y) you found by dragging. Before moving on, think about (or discuss with your group):

Why would the areas of those two swapped-in parallelograms, divided by the reference parallelogram, ever have anything to do with where a point lands when you combine î and ĵ?

If you swapped in the target for î and ĵ at the same time instead of one at a time, what do you think would happen?

Try writing your own answer first — then check it against this.

Swapping the target in for î replaces one column with the exact combination you're looking for, x·î + y·ĵ. Area scales separately with each column — pairing that combination against the untouched ĵ, the ĵ-part of it contributes nothing (a parallelogram of ĵ paired with ĵ has zero area), so all that's left over is x times the reference area. Dividing by the reference area cancels that scaling and leaves x alone. Swapping î in instead, and pairing against the target's own x·î + y·ĵ, does the mirror-image thing and leaves y.

Swapping the target in for both î and ĵ at once would compare the target against itself — and a parallelogram spanned by any vector and that same vector always has zero area, no matter what the target is. That's exactly why the method only works one column at a time: swapping just one column keeps the other original column standing there to pair against, which is what actually carries the leftover information.

Wrap-up

The general rule

What you found by comparing areas already has a name — Cramer's Rule.

The reference parallelogram is spanned by î=(a, c) and ĵ=(b, d) — its area is exactly det(M) = ad − bc, the same determinant from the area-and-determinant activity earlier in this module.

Swapping the target in for î gives a new determinant, det([target, ĵ]) — divide that by det(M) and you get x, every time.

Swapping the target in for ĵ gives det([î, target]) — divide that by det(M) and you get y. No dragging, no elimination — just three areas.
