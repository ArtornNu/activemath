The big idea: a circle is a "balanced" ellipse

Picture a loop of string looped around two pins stuck in a board, pulled taut with a pencil. As you slide the pencil around, keeping the string tight, it traces a smooth, closed curve — an ellipse. Now push the two pins together until they meet at a single point. The curve you trace becomes a perfect circle.

💡 Key insight for students

A circle isn't a different shape from an ellipse — it's the special case where the two foci (the "pins") land on exactly the same point. Every circle is an ellipse; not every ellipse is a circle.

4

From string-and-pins to an equation

The two pins are called the foci (singular: focus), F₁ and F₂. The length of the string is fixed, so as the pencil moves, one quantity never changes:

PF₁ + PF₂ = 2a   (constant, for every point P on the ellipse)

This is the locus definition of an ellipse: the set of all points whose distances to two fixed points add up to a constant. Placing the foci at (−c, 0) and (c, 0) and working through the distance formula and some algebra leads to the familiar standard form:

x²/a² + y²/b² = 1    where  b² = a² − c²

Notice what happens as c → 0 (the foci slide together): b² → a², so b = a, and the equation becomes x² + y² = a² — the equation of a circle. This is the same conclusion as the string-and-pins picture, now written in algebra.

👩‍🏫 Facilitator note

Resist the urge to present the standard-form equation first. Every activity below starts from the physical/geometric definition and lets the equation emerge — this mirrors how you'll want your own students to experience it.

Facilitator guide: running this back home

Common misconception to watch for: students often think the foci are "centers" of the ellipse, or that the ellipse has two centers. Emphasize there is exactly one center (the midpoint of F₁F₂) and two foci.

Connecting to TPACK (Module 3): notice how each tool plays a different pedagogical role — Desmos for dynamic/numeric exploration, Polypad for concrete/manipulative construction, Graspable Math for symbolic fluency. Choosing which tool to use when is itself a TPACK decision, not a technology preference.

Adapting for lower-bandwidth classrooms: all three tools can run from a single shared device projected at the front if 1-to-1 devices aren't available — the reasoning discussion matters more than everyone clicking individually.
